Trace a Drop

A student's guide to following water from your click to the ocean

Have you ever wondered where a raindrop goes after it hits the ground? Does it end up in a creek? A big river? The ocean? With Trace a Drop, you can find out — by dropping a virtual raindrop anywhere in the continental United States and watching its whole journey.

Open the app →

What is a watershed?

A watershed is all the land where water drains to the same place — like a giant funnel made of hills and valleys. If rain falls anywhere inside that funnel, it eventually flows to the same stream, river, or lake.

Every spot on land is part of a watershed. Even your school!

How to use the app

  1. Click anywhere on the map

    Pick a spot in the continental U.S. — near your town, in the mountains, wherever you're curious about. That click is your raindrop.

  2. Watch what appears

    The app talks to real scientific data from the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) — the same data real hydrologists use. In a few seconds you'll see:

    • A cyan dot — where your drop landed.
    • A dashed cyan line — your drop sliding downhill over the land until it reaches the nearest stream.
    • A green shaded area — the watershed: all the land upstream that drains past your drop's stream.
    • A bold blue line — your drop's journey downstream, from stream to river to the ocean. (Sometimes a drop ends at a lake with no outlet, like the Great Salt Lake — not every drop makes it to the sea!)

  3. Hover over the green watershed

    A popup tells you the watershed's name and its HUC code. HUC stands for Hydrologic Unit Code — it's like a ZIP code for watersheds. Scientists use these codes to keep track of every watershed in the country.

Cool things to try

Turn layers on and off

Use the checkboxes in the panel to show or hide the watershed, the downstream path, or the drop point. Turning layers off makes it easier to study one thing at a time.

Go 3D!

Click "View in 3D terrain" to tilt the map and see actual mountains and valleys. Now you can see why the water flows where it does — water always runs downhill. Click the button again to flip back to the flat 2D map.

Be a divide detective

Click on one side of a mountain range, then on the other side just a few miles away. Do the two drops end up in totally different oceans? The invisible line between them is a drainage divide. The most famous one, the Continental Divide, runs down the Rocky Mountains.

Race to the sea

Which takes a longer path to the ocean: a drop in Montana or a drop in Florida? Try both and compare the blue lines.

Find your home watershed

Click near where you live. What's your watershed's name? Where does your water end up?

If something doesn't work

Nothing happened after clicking? Make sure you clicked inside the continental United States — the data only covers that area.
Want to start over? Click the "Clear map" button.
Map looks stuck? Wait a moment — the app is asking real USGS computers for data, and sometimes that takes a few seconds.

Why this matters

Everything that happens on the land in a watershed — farms, cities, forests, litter — affects the water flowing through it. When you know your watershed, you know whose water you share. Scientists, farmers, and city planners all use maps like this one to keep rivers clean and communities safe.

So go ahead — drop a drop, and follow the water. 🌊
